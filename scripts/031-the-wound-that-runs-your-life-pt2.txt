In part one, we explored how an old wound quietly takes the wheel of your life. How the psyche locks away what was hurt and builds everything around avoiding that pain again. Now the question becomes, how do you take the wheel back? Jung called it making the unconscious conscious. The first step is recognition. Not understanding. Just seeing. Seeing that the voice calling you lazy might be protecting you from the memory of a time you gave everything and it was not enough. Seeing that choosing unavailable people might be your way of keeping love at a safe distance. The second step is harder. It is turning toward the part you locked away and speaking to it. Not with analysis. With tenderness. Jung believed the abandoned parts of the psyche do not want to be explained. They want to be acknowledged. They want someone to come back to the room where they were left and say, I see you. I am sorry. The part that feels most broken often carries your deepest gifts. Your passion. Your capacity to love fully. These did not vanish. They were placed behind a locked door. And the key is sitting with what hurts long enough for it to trust you again. That is individuation. Not becoming someone new. But reuniting with the one you left behind. What part of yourself have you been afraid to sit with?
