There is a version of you that exists only on screens. It knows what to say, when to smile, and how to make its life look like a story worth following. Jung never saw a smartphone, but he understood this danger deeply. He called it the Persona, the social mask we wear to navigate the world. The problem is not that you have one. Everyone does. The problem is when you forget you are wearing it. When the curated version of yourself becomes more real to you than the person lying awake at three in the morning wondering why none of it feels like enough. Every filter is a small agreement to hide. Every performance of happiness is a quiet betrayal of the sadness underneath. And the longer you live inside the role, the harder it becomes to remember who you were before you started playing it. Jung warned that Persona identification is one of the most common traps of modern life. You become so good at the performance that you lose access to the performer. But the performer is still there. Tired, maybe. Forgotten, perhaps. But still breathing beneath the surface. When did the role start feeling more real than the person underneath?
