There is a life you were supposed to live. Not the one you were told to want. The one that has been quietly waiting inside you since you were young enough to dream without permission. Jung called the journey toward that life individuation. He believed it was the central task of every human life. But most people get it wrong. Individuation is not about building a better you. It is about uncovering the one that was always there, buried beneath decades of adaptation. We spend the first half of life learning what the world rewards. Which feelings are safe. Which parts of ourselves earn love. And we become remarkably good at being what is expected. But somewhere around the middle of life, the mask stops fitting. A quiet despair moves in. Everything you built should feel like enough, and it does not. Because the person who built it was following a blueprint drawn by fear, not by truth. The call to become yourself arrives as a restlessness no achievement can cure. A fatigue no vacation can touch. Jung believed this call comes to everyone. The question is whether you have the courage to answer it. Not by destroying what you have built. But by finally including what you left behind. What have you been leaving behind?
